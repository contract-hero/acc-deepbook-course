The order lifecycle, end to end

Open a BalanceManager, fund it, rest a bid, inspect & cancel, then take liquidity with a market buy — fills settle back into the account, not your wallet.

1Open the account — the BalanceManager lifecycle

Order management needs an account the protocol can lock funds in. That's a BalanceManager — a shared object — and creating one is a precise three-step dance.

Key moment: the create → extract-id → re-create the client with the BM sequence. Skip step 3 and every later balanceManagerKey lookup throws — the first client has no BM registered. The create call is a thunk: createAndShareBalanceManager()(tx).

2Deposit, then rest a bid that doesn't fill

Move SUI into the BM, then place a limit bid priced low enough to rest on the book instead of crossing. A resting order is what you'll list and cancel next.

Key moment: deposit before the order (DeepBook locks the quote out of the BM, not your wallet), and price the bid at 50% of mid snapped to TICK so it can't cross. Price it at/above mid and it fills — and Section 3 would find zero open orders.

3Inspect the book, then clear it

Prove the bid is resting: query the BM's open orders, then cancel every one in a single transaction — releasing the locked funds back into the account.

Key moment: accountOpenOrders returns your order IDs (proof the bid rests); cancelAllOrders clears them in one PTB and releases the funds DeepBook had locked back into the BM. Orders are queryable on-chain objects you own through the account — not fire-and-forget messages.

4Take liquidity, and reconcile settled vs. owed

A market buy fills immediately against the maker's asks. Where do the bought coins land? Not your wallet — they settle into the BalanceManager.

Key moment: deposit + placeMarketOrder in one PTB; payWithDeep: false because DEEP_SUI is whitelisted. The fill settles into the BM's balance — that's the settled-vs-owed reconciliation: the coins are yours and credited to the account immediately, distinct from being withdrawn to your wallet.

5Drive it, and keep the gate honest

Wire the lifecycle into a five-button smoke driver and run the live suite — and understand the two guards that let it pass on a fresh sandbox without hiding real bugs.

Key moment: two guards keep the suite honest — withRetry around midPrice absorbs the sandbox's intermittent SimulateTransaction blips, and the market-buy test's narrow catch tolerates only liquidity-shaped errors or a MoveAbort. A bare gas/version VMError must still fail — catching everything would make a broken integration green.
